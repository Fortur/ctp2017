Conclusions:

  1. The distribution of companies by credit rating levels by rating agencies obviously depends on the given company’s transparency ratio8: the higher the transparency coefficient, the higher the creditworthiness level. Thus, companies of the 1st creditworthiness level are leaders in transparency among other creditworthiness levels, although they are located in the average range of 3.81. In 2017, companies of the 4th creditworthiness level are located in a low transparency range - 2.33. The companies of Premium, I and II transparency levels take the creditworthiness conditional level 3 at the lowest.

    Among companies of high creditworthiness level, we still see the non-transparent entities (of the transparency level V), although the share thereof is less than 5% (apparently, in this case other factors not related to transparency are at work). It should be noted that companies of the 4th, 5th and 6th creditworthiness levels disclose information on their operations at the transparency level IV – that is, in strict compliance with the requirements of the RF Central Bank. Accordingly, non-transparent companies are more likely to find themselves at the 4th and lower creditworthiness levels.

  2. The companies that compile their statements in compliance with international standards are more transparent and, as a result, more attractive to investors. This conclusion can be drawn from the fact that the creditworthiness level of companies, which follow international standards in disclosing their information, is higher than the given level of those who disclose their data in excess of the RF Central Bank requirements, and meanwhile do not take the guidance of standards. So, at creditworthiness level 1, 19 of 32 companies follow the accepted reporting standards, while at level 2 the same is being done only by 6 of 21 companies with the same credit rating, and at creditworthiness level 3 – only by 2 of 14 peer companies. Hence, by disclosing and verifying information in compliance with international standards, the company would build up its chances to proceed from the lower to the higher credit rating levels.

Corporate Transparency of the Major Listed Companies

Conclusions:

  1. Listed companies are more transparent than non-listing. The transparency ratio of the listed companies is significantly higher than such ratio of non-listed companies, which lies in the average range and amounts to 2.36. Non-listed companies account for 77% (539 out of 699) of the entire list of non-transparent companies (at transparency level V). These are the companies that do not disclose their public accounting information in compliance with the RF Central Bank requirements. Thus, we can say that the stock-exchange is one of the key institutions that cause a significant impact on the corporate transparency and create conditions / incentives to increase transparency. For non-listed companies, so far there are no incentives to increase their transparency, which incentives would be comparable in scale with the institute of stock-exchanges.
  2. Among the companies listed on the Moscow Stock Exchange, there are companies of transparency level V (zero). In 2017, there were 25 such companies (out of 257 listed). Public reports of these companies were not found in either Russian or English until December 22, 2017. The absence of non-transparent companies on the Moscow Stock Exchange (with rare exceptions) can be explained by the operation of mechanisms encouraging the listed companies to disclose information at the level of the RF Central Bank's requirements. First, the Listing Regulations of the Moscow Stock Exchange include Article 20 "Monitoring of disclosure requirements". Second, in compliance with the recommendations of the CJSC MICEX Stock Exchange, the Listing Committee, in order to implement measures to protect the investors' rights and to reduce the risks of investors' financial losses from the acquisition of securities with an unpredictable level of investment risk at MICEX, established the “Sector of companies with increased investment risk". This sector includes securities of those companies, which violate the requirements of the Regulation on Disclosure of Information by Securities Issuers No. 454-P, approved by the Bank of Russia on December 30, 2014, and which do not eliminate the revealed violations within the time allotted. Securities included in the "Sector of companies with increased investment risk" are specifically identified in the List of securities admitted to trading in CJSC "MICEX Stock Exchange".
  3. Companies listed both on the Moscow Stock Exchange and foreign stock-exchange markets are more transparent that the companies listed only in the Moscow Stock Exchange or only on foreign stock-exchange markets. The transparency coefficient of companies with a "double listing" amounts to 3.51. Thus, at the transparency levels from Premium to III, the aggregate share of companies with "double listing" amounts to 48%. Among them. there is one non-transparent company (transparency level V /zero/), which published its public report only in English, and in the study such reports are not taken into account. As for the rest companies, this result is quite expectable. Companies with a "double listing" prepare an annual report, which contains the blocks of information as required by the RF Central Bank as well as by the Moscow Stock Exchange and foreign stock-exchange markets.

    Russian companies listed on the London Stock Exchange (LSE) have to prepare their management reports as well as to disclose information on R&D activities and the events that occurred after the end of the financial year. Besides, the LSE sets a demand for disclosure of the corporate management reporting which should contain information regarding the internal control system, risk-management, business model and description of all management bodies and their activities (administrative, management and supervisory bodies), as well as their committees’ activities, etc.

    On its part, the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) recommends to pursue the “open-door” policy in the course of interaction with analysts, journalists, shareholders and others. Information provided on the individual or selective basis to analysts, shareholders or other persons should also be disclosed unless it has not been disclosed earlier. In 2016, the Securities and Exchange Commission (USA) initiated a public discussion of its proposals designed to broaden the requirements for information disclosure by the issuers. The main questions pertain to improvement of the “Industry Guides”, disclosure of information on the government policy (companies’ activities within the limits thereof), and sustainability (sustainable development) parameters9 . The “Industry Guides” are related to information disclosure by holding banking companies, mining companies, and limited liability companies operating in the real-estate sphere, as well as real-estate insurance and accident insurance companies operating within the oil and gas programs. The Commission also conducts the investors surveys on the ESG indices that have been already accepted by the investment community as significant for the investment valuation. Parameters in the sphere of climatic changes are considered by the Commission as obligatory for disclosure in case the company considers the same as significant, while the Commission raises the question on the efficiency of such approach. The Commission also considers it important to disclose information on the companies’ activities under the government programs being set forth in the territories of the given company’s presence and in some or another way related to its operations.

    Several international stock-exchange markets have already introduced demands on non-financial disclosures for the listed companies. The Johannesburg Stock Exchange (JSE) requires compliance with the King Code of Governance Principles (King III), which contains a provision on integrated reporting (comprehensive presentation of financial and non-financial information). The Shanghai Stock Exchange (SSE) imposes requirements for IPO applicants which include disclosure of environmental information for companies from the 14 “dirtiest” industries. The Malaysian Stock Exchange (Bursa Malaysia) sets the demands for potential issuers to disclose information on their and their subsidiaries’ activities in the sphere of CSR.
